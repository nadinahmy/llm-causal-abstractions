### ==Summary of Sander Beckers' talk==
- Representation Learning
	- Raw observed data, coming from pixels, sensors etc. and we want to learn V_H which is a set of high level variables. In other words, find a useful encoding of raw data. 
	- No causality involved here, just "transforming" X into a more useful representation
- Causal representation learning
	- not just learning high-level variables V_H
	- **U_H** — the exogenous (noise) variables at the high level
	- **V_H** — the endogenous (causal) variables at the high level
	- **M_H** — an actual SCM defined over both, i.e. the causal mechanisms connecting them
	- from raw data alone, can I recover not just _what the right variables are_, but _the causal model that governs them_?
	- the "hardest problem" because I learn variables, structure, _and_ mechanisms simultaneously, all from unstructured input.
-  CAL — Causal Abstraction Learning
	- Use (U_L, V_L, I_L) to learn M_L, U_H, V_H, M_H — such that M_H is a causal abstraction of M_L**
	- **U_L, V_L** — the low-level exogenous and endogenous variables (already identified, not learned from raw pixels)
	- **I_L** — a set of interventions at the low level [access to intervention data not just observations]
	- From these we can learn:
		- **M_L** — the actual low-level SCM (the mechanisms governing the low-level variables)
		- **U_H, V_H** — the high-level variables (similar to before)
		- **M_H** — the high-level SCM
	- **The crucial constraint, and the whole point of CAL:** M_H must be a _causal abstraction_ of M_L. M_H isn't just "some compressed high-level model," it has to satisfy the ==formal consistency condition where interventions on the high-level model correspond correctly to interventions on the low-level model==. That's what makes it an _abstraction_ rather than just an arbitrary summary.
	- Data is given in low level high dimensional entangled form
	- Our target is to obtain high level low dimensional disentangled endo & exogenous variables U_H and V_H , and also recover a causal model M_H over V_H & U_H
- We use decoders to go from high level variables to low level raw data, and an encoder to go vice versa
- We need to have some sort of "==reconstruction loss==" to measure, in the case of Smart Auto encoder
![[Pasted image 20260622003352.png|645]]
- Going from high level to low level and vice versa, we need to ensure that they are as ==close== to each other as possible ---> Notion of distance here^
- Alpha here is a threshold defining how far off / close the 2 representations should be to each other
- Standard Auto encoder only works if the latent space is independent**
- ==_"our V_H are not independent."== A standard autoencoder treats V_H as one flat vector with no internal structure — it doesn't know or care that some high-level variables might causally influence others. It's blind to the fact that V_H should really be a causal model, not just a bag of latent numbers.
- M_H , our high level causal model, maps from pairs of exogenous states and interventions, and gives you the corresponding endogenous state
- **Abstraction (τ**) : The "bridge" that groups low-level details into high-level concepts|

			- This is what makes the abstraction **mathematically precise**

- Explanation of this example as I didnt understand it at first:
	- ![[Pasted image 20260623173432.png|436]]
	- The high-level model has one variable, A. It's defined as A := X₁ + X₂, where X₁ and X₂ are the low-level variables. So the abstraction map τ just adds the two low-level variables together: give it (x₁, x₂), it returns x₁ + x₂.
	- **What ω_τ is trying to do.** Remember ω_τ takes a ==_low-level intervention_== and tries to find the _equivalent high-level intervention_. A high-level intervention here can only look like "[A ← some specific number]", because A is the only high-level variable. So ω_τ's job is: given an intervention on the X's, find the one fixed number we should force A to.
	- **The failing case: [X₁ ← 5].** This intervention says "force X₁ to be 5" — but it says _nothing_ about X₂. X₂ is left free to be whatever it naturally is. Now ask: what single number should A become? A = X₁ + X₂ = 5 + X₂. But X₂ isn't fixed, so:
		- if X₂ = 0, then A = 5
		- if X₂ = 1, then A = 6
		- if X₂ = 2, then A = 7
	- A keeps changing depending on X₂. There's no _one_ number we can lock A to that's correct no matter what X₂ does.
	- I think that saying "There is no _a_ such that _a_ = 5 + x₂ for all values of x₂" is just the same more formal way of saying: I can't pick a single constant A that works regardless of X₂.
	- **The working case: [X₁ ← x₁, X₂ ← x₂].** Now you fix _both_ low-level variables at once. Then A = x₁ + x₂ is a single, locked-in number — no free variable left to make it wobble. So ω_τ can cleanly map this to [A ← x₁ + x₂]. That's why the slide says ω_τ is defined "precisely for all" interventions of this both-variables form.
	- My takeaway from this is : **you can only lift a low-level intervention up to the high-level model if it nails the high-level variable down to a single value.** Touching X₁ alone would leave A floating (undefined); touching X₁ _and_ X₂ pins A down (defined).
	- I now have a much better understanding of this, and this simple but important example gave me a feel of how complex it can be to form a CONSISTENT abstraction that obeys all the rules for variables and interventions.
- Different abstraction approaches
	- ### Rubenstein et al. — the starting point

		**exact (τ, ω)-transformation between probabilistic models**

		This is the original notion. "Probabilistic" means the models carry probability distributions (Pr), and "exact" means the abstraction matches them perfectly — intervening then abstracting gives _exactly_ the same distribution as abstracting then intervening. This is the baseline everyone else refines.
	- Beckers & Halpern — four increasingly strict definitions , something like rungs, where the higher the rung the more requirements it adds, and the stricter it is
		- Rung 1 is uniform transformation , stop tying the abstraction to one particular distribution and demand that it hold universally.
		- Rung 2 adds 2 new requirements, **τ is surjective** — every high-level state is  reachable from a low-level state (no "orphan" abstract states the detailed model can never produce), and **𝓘_H = ω_τ(𝓘_L)** — in other words, the high-level interventions are exactly the ones you get by abstracting low-level interventions up.
		- Rung 3 is strong τ-abstraction, which adds an additional requirement that the high-level model must allow **every possible intervention** on its variables, not just a restricted subset. I guess "Strong" here = no missing interventions at the high level.
		- Rung 4 is constructive τ-abstraction, the most strict one, also seems the cleanest out of all the other rungs. Every high-level variable is abstracted from its _own private bundle_ of low-level variables, with no overlap. So in the example where A := X1+X2, we can say that the group {X₁, X₂} is one block, and τ₁(X₁, X₂) = X₁ + X₂ = A.
